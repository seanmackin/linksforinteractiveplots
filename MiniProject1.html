Kernel Methods: Mini-Project 1                    Sean Mackin

The data-set of discussion for this mini-project will be a new cut through the American Community Survey (ACS) data, in exploration of the following question:

 

How well can we predict internet access as a function of rurality and income?

The motivations for these predictors are as follows: rural areas (in general) will have a much farther distance to hubs of information infrastructure, and are thus less likely to have things such as broadband or fiber-optic cables built all the way to their homes (as compared to those living in an urban area); even if the infrastructure has made it to one’s home, there is a minimum amount of money/capital required to purchase compatible and up-to-date technology to utilize it, and thus income is likely to be a factor.

These motivations also suggest interaction between the two predictors, and thus we expect a nonlinear kernel regression model to outperform a simple linear regression model. We will operationalise rurality via population density.

As a reminder, we’d like to be able to predict internet access as a function of population density and median household income at the Census tract level, and we suspect that nonlinear kernelized regression will do a better job than standard linear regression. Of course, we don’t know which kernel will produce the best fit to the data, so we ought to test a variety of them. Preliminarily, we would suspect that an interpolative kernel would do well in this type of scenario (where the response is constrained between 0 and 1). We tested this following kernels over a range of parameters to see which performed best, and these are the lowest reported values for root mean squared error over all the parameters:

The RMSE for a linear regression with an intercept was 13.0, so we can see that the inhomogeneous polynomial, rational quadratic, and neural-net kernels are all outperforming a standard OLS regression. These results suggest that we may see even better model performance if we take a composite of an interpolative kernel and and polynomial-style kernel:

Qualitatively, we can see that income is the far more influential variable when it comes to predicting internet access, although the subtle variation in lower access in poorer, more rural regions is evident in the prediction surface.

For future research, one might consider modelling this as a kernelized logistic regression, given that the model is measuring a quantity between 0 and 1. Also, investigating the time-series of the Census/ACS data to see whether the more rural areas in the past were slower to get access to internet services would be insightful, since the adoption of the Internet in 2020 is pretty large. Also, one might consider weighting the census tracts in some way as to not count them all equally but in proportion to population, as this could reveal some kind of mucky dependence on the geometries of Census tracts used.
